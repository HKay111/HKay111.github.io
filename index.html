Undergraduate Thesis: Carbon Emissions in Africa: EKC and Regional Integration

This thesis examines two interconnected questions using a panel of nine African countries — Algeria, Kenya, Mauritius, Morocco, Mozambique, Namibia, Nigeria, South Africa, and Zambia — from 2006 to 2022.

Paper 1: This paper examines the impact of regional integration on carbon emissions in the African region, using a panel of nine countries. The impact of regional integration is investigated through regional value chains, environmental co-operation, and institutional integration. The study uses panel techniques, such as the fully modified OLS estimator, which accounts for cross-sectional dependence (CSD), non-stationarity, and endogeneity. The study finds co-integration relationships between its variables. The results indicate that trade openness and regional value chains increase consumption-based emissions, suggesting that in lower-income and developing economies, people purchase goods and merchandise despite the environmental impact to meet their consumption needs. Environmental Co-operation and institutional integration reduce emissions, reinforcing the idea that better co-operation between countries and their governments helps combat pollution, and that better trade deals reduce countries' emissions. The study also reveals a significant impact of the government effectiveness indicator on emissions, indicating that effective government intervention and policy implementation reduce emissions. The results of the study indicate that Gross Domestic Product (GDP) and energy use (per capita) positively impact emissions, implying that as a country develops, it engages in more emission-intensive activity.

Paper 2: This study examines the existence of an Environmental Kuznets Curve (EKC) across a set of environmentally heterogeneous African countries. To account for cross-sectional dependence (CSD), non-stationarity, and endogeneity, the analysis employs a group-mean Fully Modified Ordinary Least Squares (FMOLS) estimator. The results confirm the presence of a long-run cointegrating relationship among the variables and provide evidence of slope heterogeneity. Contrary to the conventional inverted-U EKC hypothesis, the findings indicate a U-shaped relationship between income and environmental degradation. Specifically, carbon emissions per capita initially decline with rising income levels but begin to increase beyond a certain income threshold. Furthermore, the estimates reveal an approximately one-to-one elasticity between energy use per capita and carbon emissions per capita, suggesting a strong reliance on carbon-intensive energy sources. These findings imply that, without structural changes in the energy mix, further economic growth may exacerbate environmental degradation. Accordingly, there is a pressing need for policy interventions to promote a transition to cleaner, more sustainable energy sources across the region.
