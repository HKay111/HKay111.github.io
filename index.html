Undergraduate Thesis: Carbon Emissions in Africa: EKC and Regional Integration

This thesis examines two interconnected questions using a panel of nine African countries — Algeria, Kenya, Mauritius, Morocco, Mozambique, Namibia, Nigeria, South Africa, and Zambia — from 2006 to 2022.

Main Paper: Investigates the impact of regional integration on carbon emissions through regional value chains, environmental co-operation, and institutional integration. Using the Fully Modified OLS estimator (accounting for cross-sectional dependence, non-stationarity, and endogeneity), the study finds co-integration relationships and shows that trade openness and regional value chains increase consumption-based emissions — suggesting that in lower-income and developing economies, people purchase goods and merchandise despite the environmental impact to meet consumption needs. Environmental Co-operation and institutional integration reduce emissions, reinforcing that better co-operation between countries and their governments helps combat pollution. The study also finds a significant negative effect of the government effectiveness indicator on emissions, indicating that effective government intervention reduces pollution.

Side Paper: Examines the Environmental Kuznets Curve (EKC) across the same countries. Contrary to the conventional inverted-U EKC hypothesis, findings indicate a U-shaped relationship — carbon emissions per capita first decline with rising income, then increase beyond a certain threshold. Energy use per capita shows approximately one-to-one elasticity with carbon emissions per capita, suggesting a strong reliance on carbon-intensive energy sources. Both papers employ the group-mean FMOLS estimator and confirm the presence of long-run cointegrating relationships with slope heterogeneity.

My research sits at the intersection of environmental and development economics, with a focus on how trade, integration, and policy affect environmental outcomes in developing economies — particularly Africa. My thesis applies panel time-series methods to study co-integration between regional integration and carbon emissions, finding that while trade openness increases emissions, stronger environmental co-operation and institutional integration reduce them. The EKC analysis reveals a U-shaped relationship, suggesting African countries may face growing emissions pressure as income rises unless structural changes in the energy mix are made.
